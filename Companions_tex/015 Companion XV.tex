\begin{abstract}
The early-Universe gravitational-wave (GW) spectrum provides a uniquely sensitive probe of the microphysical processes that shaped the primordial plasma. In the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC), the universal relaxation mechanism introduced in Companion~X modifies the evolution of tensor perturbations during the earliest stages of cosmic history. The relaxation rate $\Gamma(a) = \alpha(a) H(a)$ introduces an additional damping term in the tensor-mode equation of motion, suppressing the amplitude of primordial gravitational waves on scales that re-entered the horizon during the relaxation-dominated epoch.

Combined with the sectoral temperature asymmetry between the two CPT-conjugate sectors, this leads to a characteristic sectoral GW asymmetry and a relaxation-imprinted suppression feature in the stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB). The colder CPT sector experiences weaker damping and produces a distinct tensor spectrum, while the visible sector exhibits a relaxation-induced suppression at high frequencies.

In this Companion we derive the relaxation-corrected tensor-mode evolution, compute the resulting GW transfer function, and obtain the full early-Universe GW spectrum in RDCC. We incorporate the modernized sectoral thermodynamics from Companion~XIII, the CPT-sector PBH interpretation from Companion~XIV, and the tensor-modulation structure from Companion~II. The results establish the SGWB as a central observational probe of the relaxation-driven dynamics that define the RDCC framework.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

The stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB) generated in the early Universe is a uniquely sensitive probe of high-energy physics. In standard cosmology, the SGWB is determined by the primordial tensor spectrum and its subsequent evolution through radiation and matter domination. The resulting spectrum is smooth, nearly scale-invariant, and free of dynamical features except for small steps associated with changes in the relativistic degrees of freedom.

In the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC), the situation is fundamentally different. The universal relaxation mechanism introduced in Companion~X modifies the evolution of super-horizon tensor modes during the earliest stages of cosmic history. When the relaxation rate satisfies
\begin{equation}
\Gamma(a) = \alpha(a) H(a) \gtrsim H(a),
\end{equation}
tensor modes experience an additional damping term that suppresses their amplitude before horizon re-entry. This effect leaves a characteristic imprint on the SGWB: a smooth, power-law suppression at frequencies corresponding to modes that re-entered during the relaxation-dominated epoch.

A second key ingredient is the sectoral temperature asymmetry between the two CPT-conjugate sectors. As shown in Companion~X, the temperature ratio
\begin{equation}
\frac{T_B}{T_A} \approx 0.5
\end{equation}
implies different relaxation histories, horizon masses, and damping scales in the two sectors. Tensor modes in the colder CPT sector experience weaker damping, leading to a sectoral GW asymmetry.

This Companion develops the early-Universe gravitational-wave sector of RDCC, incorporating the modernized sectoral thermodynamics from Companion~XIII, the CPT-sector PBH interpretation from Companion~XIV, and the tensor-modulation structure from Companion~II. The SGWB emerges as a central observational probe of the relaxation-driven dynamics that define the RDCC framework.

\section{Tensor Modes in Standard Cosmology}

In standard cosmology, primordial tensor perturbations obey the equation
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_k + 3H \dot{h}_k + \frac{k^2}{a^2} h_k = 0,
\end{equation}
with two distinct regimes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Super-horizon ($k < aH$):} Tensor modes freeze out with constant amplitude.
\item \textbf{Sub-horizon ($k > aH$):} Tensor modes oscillate with frequency $k/a$ and decay as $a^{-1}$ due to Hubble friction.
\end{itemize}

The primordial tensor spectrum is parameterized as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_h(k) = A_t \left(\frac{k}{k_*}\right)^{n_t},
\end{equation}
and the present-day GW energy density is
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(k) = \frac{1}{12} \left(\frac{k}{a_0 H_0}\right)^2 \mathcal{P}_h(k) T^2(k),
\end{equation}
where $T(k)$ is the transfer function.

Crucially, standard cosmology contains no additional damping beyond Hubble friction. The SGWB is therefore smooth and featureless, making it exceptionally sensitive to any new early-Universe physics.

\section{Tensor Modes in RDCC: Relaxation-Induced Damping}

In RDCC, the universal relaxation mechanism modifies the tensor-mode equation by introducing an additional damping term. The evolution equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_k + \left(3H + \Gamma(a)\right)\dot{h}_k + \frac{k^2}{a^2} h_k = 0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\Gamma(a) = \alpha(a) H(a),
\end{equation}
where $\alpha(a)$ is the relaxation coefficient derived in Companion~X.

\subsection{Super-Horizon Evolution}

For $k < aH$, the equation reduces to
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_k + (3 + \alpha) H \dot{h}_k \approx 0.
\end{equation}
During radiation domination ($H \propto a^{-2}$), the solution is
\begin{equation}
h_k(a) \propto a^{-\alpha/2}.
\end{equation}

Thus, super-horizon tensor modes are suppressed by
\begin{equation}
S(a) = \left(\frac{a}{a_{\rm in}}\right)^{-\alpha/2},
\end{equation}
where $a_{\rm in}$ marks the onset of the relaxation-dominated epoch.

\subsection{Sub-Horizon Evolution}

After horizon re-entry ($k > aH$), the equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_k + (3H + \Gamma)\dot{h}_k + \frac{k^2}{a^2} h_k = 0.
\end{equation}
The amplitude decays as
\begin{equation}
h_k(a) \propto a^{-1} \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\int_{a_{\rm re}}^{a} \alpha(a') \frac{da'}{a'}\right].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Connection to Tensor Modulation (Companion II)}

Companion~II shows that the same inter-sectoral relaxation dynamics induce a modulation of the tensor sector, producing log-periodic features in the primordial tensor spectrum. The relaxation-induced damping derived here is the \emph{late-time} manifestation of the same mechanism. Both effects originate from the bipartite CPT structure and the \(\alpha\)-controlled inter-sectoral coupling.

\subsection{Sectoral Asymmetry}

Because the CPT-conjugate sector is colder,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_B(a) < \alpha_A(a),
\end{equation}
tensor modes in sector B experience weaker damping:
\begin{equation}
h_k^{(B)} > h_k^{(A)}.
\end{equation}

This sectoral GW asymmetry parallels the sectoral PBH asymmetry derived in Companion~XIV and is a direct consequence of the RDCC relaxation dynamics.

In the next section, we compute the relaxation-corrected transfer function and derive the full SGWB spectrum.

\section{Relaxation-Corrected Transfer Function}

The transfer function $T(k)$ encodes the evolution of tensor modes from their primordial generation to the present day. In standard cosmology, $T(k)$ is determined by horizon re-entry, changes in the relativistic degrees of freedom, and the transition from radiation to matter domination. In the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC), the universal relaxation mechanism introduces an additional suppression factor that modifies the transfer function on scales that re-entered the horizon during the relaxation-dominated epoch.

\subsection{Definition}

The present-day tensor power spectrum is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_h(k, a_0) = \mathcal{P}_h(k, a_{\rm prim})\, T^2(k),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}_h(k, a_{\rm prim})$ is the primordial tensor spectrum and $T(k)$ encodes all subsequent evolution.

In RDCC, the transfer function factorizes as
\begin{equation}
T(k) = T_{\rm std}(k)\, T_{\rm rel}(k),
\end{equation}
where $T_{\rm std}(k)$ is the standard transfer function and $T_{\rm rel}(k)$ captures the relaxation-induced suppression.

\subsection{Relaxation-Induced Suppression}

From the tensor-mode evolution derived in Section~3, the amplitude of a tensor mode is suppressed by two effects:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Super-horizon suppression} by the factor $S(a)$,
\item \textbf{Enhanced sub-horizon damping} due to the additional friction term $\Gamma(a)$.
\end{itemize}

The combined suppression factor is
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm rel}(k) = S(a_{\rm re}) \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\int_{a_{\rm re}}^{a_{\rm end}} \alpha(a)\,\frac{da}{a}\right],
\end{equation}
where $a_{\rm re}$ is the scale factor at horizon re-entry and $a_{\rm end}$ marks the end of the relaxation-dominated epoch.

Using $\Gamma(a) = \alpha(a) H(a)$, the integral simplifies to
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm rel}(k) \simeq S(a_{\rm re}) \left(\frac{a_{\rm re}}{a_{\rm end}}\right)^{\alpha/2},
\end{equation}
if $\alpha(a)$ varies slowly.

\subsection{Scale Dependence}

Relaxation affects only modes that re-entered the horizon while $\Gamma(a) \ge H(a)$. Defining the characteristic scale $k_{\rm rel}$ by
\begin{equation}
k_{\rm rel} = a_{\rm rel} H(a_{\rm rel}),
\end{equation}
where $a_{\rm rel}$ satisfies $\Gamma(a_{\rm rel}) = H(a_{\rm rel})$, we obtain:

\begin{itemize}
\item $k \ll k_{\rm rel}$: Modes re-enter after relaxation ends. No suppression: $T_{\rm rel}(k) \approx 1$.
\item $k \gg k_{\rm rel}$: Modes re-enter during relaxation. Strong suppression: $T_{\rm rel}(k) \propto k^{-\alpha/2}$.
\end{itemize}

Thus, the relaxation mechanism introduces a power-law suppression feature in the SGWB spectrum at high frequencies.

\subsection{Sectoral Asymmetry}

Because the two CPT-conjugate sectors have different temperatures,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_B(a) < \alpha_A(a),
\end{equation}
their relaxation histories differ. The colder CPT sector experiences weaker damping:
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm rel}^{(B)}(k) > T_{\rm rel}^{(A)}(k).
\end{equation}

This sectoral GW asymmetry parallels the sectoral PBH asymmetry derived in Companion~XIV and is a direct consequence of the RDCC relaxation dynamics.

\subsection{Resulting Transfer Function}

Combining the standard and relaxation-induced contributions yields
\begin{equation}
T(k) = T_{\rm std}(k)\, S(a_{\rm re}) \exp\left[-\frac{1}{2}\int_{a_{\rm re}}^{a_{\rm end}} \alpha(a)\,\frac{da}{a}\right].
\end{equation}

This transfer function forms the basis for computing the full stochastic gravitational-wave background in RDCC.

\section{The Early-Universe GW Spectrum in RDCC}

With the relaxation-corrected transfer function derived in Section~4, we now compute the full stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB) in the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC). The resulting spectrum exhibits a characteristic suppression feature at high frequencies, reflecting the damping of tensor modes during the relaxation-dominated epoch.

\subsection{General Expression}

The present-day GW energy density per logarithmic interval in wavenumber is
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(k) = \frac{1}{12}\left(\frac{k}{a_0 H_0}\right)^2 \mathcal{P}_h(k)\, T^2(k),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
T(k) = T_{\rm std}(k)\, T_{\rm rel}(k).
\end{equation}

The primordial spectrum is parameterized as
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P}_h(k) = A_t \left(\frac{k}{k_*}\right)^{n_t}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Relaxation-Induced Suppression Feature}

Using the relaxation-corrected transfer function, the GW spectrum becomes
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(k) = \Omega_{\rm std}(k)\, T_{\rm rel}^2(k),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm rel}(k) \approx
\begin{cases}
1, & k \ll k_{\rm rel}, \\
\left(\frac{k}{k_{\rm rel}}\right)^{-\alpha/2}, & k \gg k_{\rm rel}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Thus, the relaxation mechanism introduces a power-law suppression at high frequencies:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(k) \propto k^{n_t - \alpha}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Frequency Range of the Suppression Feature}

The characteristic frequency associated with $k_{\rm rel}$ is
\begin{equation}
f_{\rm rel} = \frac{k_{\rm rel}}{2\pi a_0}.
\end{equation}

For typical RDCC parameters, the relaxation-dominated epoch occurs at temperatures
\begin{equation}
T \sim 10^7 - 10^{10}\,{\rm GeV},
\end{equation}
corresponding to frequencies
\begin{equation}
f_{\rm rel} \sim 10^{-3} - 10^{2}\,{\rm Hz}.
\end{equation}

This range overlaps with the sensitivity windows of LISA, DECIGO, and BBO.

\subsection{Sectoral GW Asymmetry}

Because $\alpha_B < \alpha_A$, the colder CPT sector has a shallower suppression slope:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}^{(B)}(k) > \Omega_{\rm GW}^{(A)}(k)
\end{equation}
for $k \gg k_{\rm rel}$.

\subsection{Full RDCC GW Spectrum}

Combining all contributions, the RDCC GW spectrum takes the form
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(k) =
\frac{1}{12}
\left(\frac{k}{a_0 H_0}\right)^2
A_t
\left(\frac{k}{k_*}\right)^{n_t}
T_{\rm std}^2(k)
T_{\rm rel}^2(k),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
T_{\rm rel}(k) \approx
\begin{cases}
1, & k \ll k_{\rm rel}, \\
\left(\frac{k}{k_{\rm rel}}\right)^{-\alpha/2}, & k \gg k_{\rm rel}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

This spectrum exhibits a smooth, nearly scale-invariant low-frequency regime and a relaxation-induced suppression at high frequencies. The location and slope of the suppression feature provide direct information about the relaxation dynamics and the temperature asymmetry between the two sectors.

\section{Observational Predictions}

The relaxation-corrected gravitational-wave (GW) spectrum derived in Section~5 leads to a set of concrete and testable predictions. These predictions arise from the interplay between relaxation-induced tensor damping, the sectoral temperature asymmetry, and the frequency dependence of horizon re-entry. Together, they provide a powerful observational handle on the relaxation-driven dynamics that define the RDCC framework.

\subsection{Prediction 1: High-Frequency Suppression Feature}

The universal relaxation mechanism introduces a characteristic suppression feature in the stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB) at frequencies
\begin{equation}
f \gtrsim f_{\rm rel} \sim 10^{-3} - 10^{2}\,{\rm Hz}.
\end{equation}
Modes that re-entered the horizon during the relaxation-dominated epoch experience a power-law suppression,
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) \propto f^{\,n_t - \alpha},
\end{equation}
with $\alpha$ the relaxation coefficient. This feature is absent in standard cosmology and provides a direct probe of relaxation-driven early-Universe physics.

\subsection{Prediction 2: Observable Break Scale}

The break frequency $f_{\rm rel}$ marks the transition between the unsuppressed and suppressed regimes. For typical RDCC parameters, $f_{\rm rel}$ lies within the sensitivity windows of LISA, DECIGO, and BBO. A detection of a spectral break at this frequency would provide strong evidence for the relaxation mechanism.

\subsection{Prediction 3: Modified Spectral Slope}

Above the break scale, the GW spectrum exhibits a modified slope,
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\ln \Omega_{\rm GW}}{d\ln f} = n_t - \alpha,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = \mathcal{O}(0.1 - 1)$ during the relaxation epoch. This leads to a significant deviation from the nearly scale-invariant spectrum predicted by standard cosmology.

\subsection{Prediction 4: Sectoral GW Asymmetry}

Because the two CPT-conjugate sectors have different temperatures,
\begin{equation}
\alpha_B < \alpha_A,
\end{equation}
their relaxation histories differ. This implies a sectoral asymmetry in the GW spectrum,
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}^{(B)}(f) > \Omega_{\rm GW}^{(A)}(f)
\end{equation}
for $f \gtrsim f_{\rm rel}$. While only the visible-sector spectrum is directly observable, the mirror-sector spectrum affects the global energy budget and provides an internal consistency check of the RDCC framework.

\subsection{Prediction 5: Consistency with PBH Constraints}

The relaxation-induced suppression of tensor modes is consistent with the suppression of primordial black hole (PBH) formation derived in Companion~XIV. Both effects originate from the same relaxation dynamics and occur at similar energy scales. A joint analysis of PBH constraints and GW observations can therefore test the internal coherence of RDCC.

\subsection{Prediction 6: Distinguishability from Other New-Physics Scenarios}

The relaxation-induced suppression feature differs qualitatively from other mechanisms that modify the SGWB, such as:
\begin{itemize}
\item changes in relativistic degrees of freedom ($g_*$),
\item phase transitions,
\item cosmic strings,
\item reheating effects.
\end{itemize}
These scenarios typically produce sharp features, bumps, or oscillations, whereas RDCC predicts a smooth power-law suppression with a well-defined break scale. This distinct spectral shape allows the relaxation mechanism to be distinguished from other early-Universe physics.

\subsection{Prediction 7: Direct Testability with Future Detectors}

The predicted suppression feature lies in the frequency range accessible to upcoming space-based interferometers. In particular:
\begin{itemize}
\item LISA can probe the low-frequency edge of the suppression feature,
\item DECIGO and BBO can probe the full suppression regime,
\item high-frequency detectors (MHz–GHz) can test the extreme tail of the spectrum.
\end{itemize}
A detection of the predicted spectral break and modified slope would provide strong evidence for the relaxation mechanism and the bipartite structure of the early Universe.

\section{Conclusion}

In this Companion we have developed the gravitational-wave (GW) sector of the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC), incorporating the modernized relaxation dynamics from Companion~X, the sectoral thermodynamics from Companion~XIII, the CPT-sector PBH interpretation from Companion~XIV, and the tensor-modulation structure from Companion~II.

The key result is that the relaxation mechanism introduces a characteristic suppression feature in the GW spectrum at frequencies corresponding to modes that re-entered the horizon during the relaxation-dominated epoch. This suppression arises from two effects: the relaxation-induced damping of super-horizon tensor modes and the enhanced friction term affecting sub-horizon evolution. The resulting spectrum exhibits a smooth power-law suppression above a well-defined break scale, providing a clear observational signature of relaxation-driven early-Universe physics.

A second important result is the sectoral GW asymmetry. Because the two CPT-conjugate sectors have different temperatures, their relaxation histories differ, leading to distinct GW spectra. This sectoral asymmetry parallels the sectoral PBH asymmetry developed in Companion~XIV and provides an internal consistency check of the RDCC framework.

With the development of the GW sector, RDCC now provides a coherent and internally consistent description of scalar, tensor, and PBH-related early-Universe physics. The SGWB emerges as a central observational probe of the relaxation-driven dynamics that define the RDCC cosmology.
