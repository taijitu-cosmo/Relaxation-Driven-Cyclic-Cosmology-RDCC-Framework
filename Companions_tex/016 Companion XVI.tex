\begin{abstract}
The Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) provides a unified framework in which the 
thermodynamic relaxation mechanism introduced in Companion~X governs the evolution of the 
early Universe, generating sectoral asymmetries, suppressing primordial black hole formation, 
modifying the gravitational-wave spectrum, and shaping the neutrino sector. To connect these 
predictions with cosmological observations, a complete Boltzmann framework is required. This 
Companion develops the RDCC Boltzmann Code: a consistent set of background and perturbation 
equations incorporating relaxation-induced modifications, sectoral temperature asymmetry, and 
the bipartite global state.

We derive the modified Friedmann equations, the sectoral entropy-flow relations, and the 
relaxation-corrected evolution of scalar, vector, and tensor perturbations. The resulting 
Boltzmann hierarchy generalizes the standard cosmological perturbation theory by including 
relaxation terms, sectoral coupling coefficients, and modified sound speeds. We provide a 
blueprint for numerical implementation, including a CLASS-compatible structure, initial 
conditions, and parameter dependencies. The RDCC Boltzmann Code forms the foundation for 
computing CMB anisotropies, matter power spectra, growth rates, non-Gaussianities, and 
gravitational-wave transfer functions within the RDCC framework.

This Companion establishes the computational backbone of RDCC and prepares the ground for 
future Companions addressing small-scale structure, cosmic magnetic fields, non-Gaussianities, 
and global parameter fits.

\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

The Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) provides a unified framework in which the

Modern cosmology relies on Boltzmann codes such as \textsc{CLASS} and \textsc{CAMB} to compute 
cosmic microwave background (CMB) anisotropies, matter power spectra, growth rates, and 
gravitational-wave transfer functions. These codes solve the coupled system of background 
evolution, linear perturbations, and Boltzmann hierarchies for photons, neutrinos, baryons, 
and dark matter. To test RDCC against observational data, an analogous framework is required 
that incorporates the relaxation mechanism and the bipartite sectoral structure.

The need for an RDCC Boltzmann code arises from three considerations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Modified background evolution:}

    sectoral coupling coefficients, altering the evolution of density contrasts, velocities, 
    and anisotropic stresses.

    \item \textbf{Sectoral temperature asymmetry:}  
    The two CPT-conjugate sectors evolve with different temperatures, sound speeds, and 
    horizon scales, requiring a generalized Boltzmann hierarchy.

A dedicated RDCC Boltzmann code enables the computation of:

    \item tensor transfer functions and the stochastic gravitational-wave background,
    \item non-Gaussianities and scale-dependent signatures,
    \item small-scale structure observables (Lyman-$\alpha$, subhalo abundance, core–cusp).

This Companion develops the RDCC Boltzmann Code: a consistent set of background and

\section{Background Evolution in RDCC}

The background evolution of the Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) differs from 
standard cosmology due to the presence of the universal relaxation mechanism and the 
bipartite sectoral structure. In this section we derive the modified Friedmann equations, the 
sectoral entropy-flow relations, and the temperature evolution in both sectors. These 
relations form the backbone of the RDCC Boltzmann framework.

\subsection*{2.1 Bipartite Sectoral Structure}

RDCC contains two CPT-conjugate sectors, denoted $A$ (visible) and $B$ (mirror), each with 
its own temperature, entropy, and energy density:
\begin{equation}
    T_A \neq T_B, 
    \qquad 
    s_A \neq s_B,
    \qquad
    \rho_A \neq \rho_B.
\end{equation}
As shown in Companion~X, the relaxation mechanism drives a persistent temperature asymmetry,
\begin{equation}
    \frac{T_B}{T_A} \simeq 0.5,
\end{equation}
which remains stable throughout the early Universe.

The total energy density is the sum of the two sectors,
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\mathrm{tot}} = \rho_A + \rho_B,
\end{equation}
and similarly for pressure and entropy.

\subsection*{2.2 Modified Friedmann Equations}

The relaxation mechanism introduces an additional term in the continuity equations of each 
sector. The Friedmann equations retain their standard form,
\begin{equation}
    H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_{\mathrm{tot}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \dot{H} = -4\pi G (\rho_{\mathrm{tot}} + p_{\mathrm{tot}}),
\end{equation}
but the evolution of $\rho_A$ and $\rho_B$ is modified by relaxation-induced energy flow.

\subsection*{2.3 Relaxation-Induced Energy Exchange}

The relaxation mechanism mediates a bidirectional but asymmetric energy exchange between the 
two sectors. The continuity equations become
\begin{align}
    \dot{\rho}_A + 3H(\rho_A + p_A) &= -\Gamma (\rho_A - \rho_B), \\
    \dot{\rho}_B + 3H(\rho_B + p_B) &= +\Gamma (\rho_A - \rho_B),
\end{align}
where $\Gamma(a)$ is the relaxation rate,
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma(a) = \alpha(a)\, H(a),
\end{equation}
with $\alpha(a)$ a slowly varying dimensionless coefficient.

The total energy density is conserved:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\rho}_{\mathrm{tot}} + 3H(\rho_{\mathrm{tot}} + p_{\mathrm{tot}}) = 0.
\end{equation}

\subsection*{2.4 Sectoral Temperature Evolution}

For each sector, the temperature evolution follows from entropy conservation modified by 
relaxation. The entropy densities satisfy
\begin{align}
    \dot{s}_A + 3H s_A &= -\frac{\Gamma}{T_A}(\rho_A - \rho_B), \\
    \dot{s}_B + 3H s_B &= +\frac{\Gamma}{T_B}(\rho_A - \rho_B).
\end{align}

Using $s \propto g_{*s} T^3$, the temperature evolution becomes
\begin{align}
    \frac{\dot{T}_A}{T_A} &= -H 
    + \frac{\Gamma}{3(\rho_A + p_A)}(\rho_A - \rho_B), \\
    \frac{\dot{T}_B}{T_B} &= -H 
    - \frac{\Gamma}{3(\rho_B + p_B)}(\rho_A - \rho_B).
\end{align}

These equations ensure that the temperature ratio $T_B/T_A$ remains stable during the 
relaxation-dominated epoch.

\subsection*{2.5 Effective Equation of State}

The relaxation mechanism modifies the effective equation of state of each sector. Defining
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \frac{p_i}{\rho_i},
\end{equation}
the effective equation of state becomes
\begin{equation}
    w_i^{\mathrm{eff}}
    = w_i 
    \mp \frac{\Gamma}{3H}
      \left( 1 - \frac{\rho_j}{\rho_i} \right),
\end{equation}
with the upper sign for sector~A and the lower sign for sector~B.

This modification affects the sound speed, the horizon scale, and the growth of perturbations.

\subsection*{2.6 Summary of Background Evolution}

The background evolution of RDCC is governed by:

\begin{itemize}
    \item standard Friedmann equations with total energy density $\rho_A + \rho_B$,
    \item relaxation-modified continuity equations for each sector,
    \item sectoral temperature evolution with stable asymmetry,
    \item effective equations of state modified by relaxation.
\end{itemize}

These relations define the background quantities required for the RDCC Boltzmann hierarchy, 
including $H(a)$, $T_A(a)$, $T_B(a)$, $w_i(a)$, and the relaxation coefficient $\alpha(a)$.

In the next section, we derive the scalar, vector, and tensor perturbation equations that form 
the core of the RDCC Boltzmann Code.

\section{Linear Perturbations in RDCC}

To compute cosmological observables such as CMB anisotropies and matter power spectra, we 
require the evolution of linear perturbations around the RDCC background. In this section we 
derive the scalar, vector, and tensor perturbation equations, including the modifications 
introduced by the relaxation mechanism and the bipartite sectoral structure.

We work in conformal Newtonian gauge,
\begin{equation}
    ds^2 = a^2(\tau)\left[
        -(1+2\Psi)d\tau^2 + (1-2\Phi)\delta_{ij}dx^i dx^j
    \right],
\end{equation}
where $\Psi$ and $\Phi$ are the scalar potentials. Vector and tensor perturbations are treated 
separately below.

\subsection*{3.1 Sectoral Perturbation Variables}

Each sector $i \in \{A,B\}$ contains its own density contrast, velocity divergence, and 
anisotropic stress:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_i = \frac{\delta\rho_i}{\rho_i}, 
    \qquad
    \theta_i = \partial_j v_i^j,
    \qquad
    \sigma_i.
\end{equation}

The relaxation mechanism introduces a coupling between the perturbations of the two sectors. 
To first order, the relaxation-induced energy exchange modifies the continuity and Euler 
equations.

\subsection*{3.2 Scalar Perturbations}

The perturbed continuity equations become
\begin{align}
    \dot{\delta}_A 
    + (1+w_A)(\theta_A - 3\dot{\Phi})
    + 3H(c_{s,A}^2 - w_A)\delta_A
    &= -\Gamma(\delta_A - \delta_B)
       - \Gamma\frac{\rho_A - \rho_B}{\rho_A}\delta_A,
    \\
    \dot{\delta}_B 
    + (1+w_B)(\theta_B - 3\dot{\Phi})
    + 3H(c_{s,B}^2 - w_B)\delta_B
    &= +\Gamma(\delta_A - \delta_B)
       - \Gamma\frac{\rho_A - \rho_B}{\rho_B}\delta_B.
\end{align}

The Euler equations become
\begin{align}
    \dot{\theta}_A 
    + H(1-3c_{s,A}^2)\theta_A
    - \frac{k^2 c_{s,A}^2}{1+w_A}\delta_A
    - k^2\Psi
    &= -\Gamma(\theta_A - \theta_B),
    \\
    \dot{\theta}_B 
    + H(1-3c_{s,B}^2)\theta_B
    - \frac{k^2 c_{s,B}^2}{1+w_B}\delta_B
    - k^2\Psi
    &= +\Gamma(\theta_A - \theta_B).
\end{align}

The relaxation terms act as a friction-like coupling between the two sectors, driving their 
perturbations toward partial equilibration.

\subsection*{3.3 Modified Einstein Equations}

The Einstein equations retain their standard form but with total energy density, pressure, and 
anisotropic stress given by
\begin{equation}
    \delta\rho_{\mathrm{tot}} = \delta\rho_A + \delta\rho_B,
    \qquad
    \delta p_{\mathrm{tot}} = \delta p_A + \delta p_B,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\mathrm{tot}} = \sigma_A + \sigma_B.
\end{equation}

The Poisson equation becomes
\begin{equation}
    k^2\Phi + 3H(\dot{\Phi} + H\Psi)
    = 4\pi G a^2 \delta\rho_{\mathrm{tot}}.
\end{equation}

The anisotropic stress relation becomes
\begin{equation}
    k^2(\Phi - \Psi)
    = 12\pi G a^2 (\rho_{\mathrm{tot}} + p_{\mathrm{tot}})\sigma_{\mathrm{tot}}.
\end{equation}

Relaxation modifies $\sigma_{\mathrm{tot}}$ indirectly through the sectoral evolution of 
$\sigma_A$ and $\sigma_B$.

\subsection*{3.4 Vector Perturbations}

Vector modes decay in standard cosmology but can be sourced by relaxation-induced 
momentum exchange. The vector perturbation equation becomes
\begin{equation}
    \dot{V}_i + 2H V_i
    = 8\pi G a^2 (\rho_{\mathrm{tot}} + p_{\mathrm{tot}})\pi_i,
\end{equation}
where $\pi_i$ is the vector anisotropic stress.

Relaxation introduces a sectoral coupling:
\begin{equation}
    \pi_i^{(A)} \propto -\Gamma (v_i^{(A)} - v_i^{(B)}),
    \qquad
    \pi_i^{(B)} \propto +\Gamma (v_i^{(A)} - v_i^{(B)}).
\end{equation}

This provides a new source of vector modes absent in standard cosmology.

\subsection*{3.5 Tensor Perturbations}

Tensor modes satisfy the relaxation-modified equation derived in Companion~XV:
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{h}_k + (3H + \Gamma)\dot{h}_k + \frac{k^2}{a^2}h_k = 0.
\end{equation}

In the Boltzmann hierarchy, this modifies the tensor transfer function and the resulting 
stochastic gravitational-wave background.

\subsection*{3.6 Summary of Perturbation Modifications}

The relaxation mechanism introduces three key modifications:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Energy exchange between sectors:}  
    Coupling terms in the continuity and Euler equations.

    \item \textbf{Momentum exchange:}  
    Relaxation-induced friction between sectoral velocities.

    \item \textbf{Tensor damping:}  
    Additional friction term in the tensor-mode equation.
\end{itemize}

These modifications propagate into the Boltzmann hierarchy for photons, neutrinos, baryons, 
and dark matter, which we develop in the next section.

\section{The RDCC Boltzmann Hierarchy}

The Boltzmann hierarchy describes the evolution of the phase-space distribution functions for 
photons, neutrinos, baryons, and dark matter. In RDCC, the hierarchy must be generalized to 
account for the bipartite sectoral structure and the relaxation-induced coupling between the 
two sectors. This section develops the full RDCC Boltzmann hierarchy, including scalar, 
vector, and tensor multipoles.

\subsection*{4.1 Distribution Functions and Sectoral Structure}

For each species $X$ in sector $i \in \{A,B\}$ we define the perturbed distribution function
\begin{equation}
    f_{X,i}(\tau, \vec{x}, \vec{q})
    = f^{(0)}_{X,i}(q,\tau)\left[1 + \Psi_{X,i}(\tau,\vec{x},\hat{n})\right],
\end{equation}
where $\hat{n}$ is the direction of propagation and $\Psi_{X,i}$ encodes the perturbation.

The relaxation mechanism introduces a coupling between the perturbations of the two sectors:
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{X,A} \leftrightarrow \Psi_{X,B}
    \quad \text{via} \quad \Gamma(a).
\end{equation}

\subsection*{4.2 Scalar Boltzmann Hierarchy}

Expanding $\Psi_{X,i}$ in Legendre multipoles,
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{X,i}(\mu) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{\infty} (-i)^\ell (2\ell+1)\Psi_{\ell,i} P_\ell(\mu),
\end{equation}
the Boltzmann hierarchy becomes
\begin{align}
    \dot{\Psi}_{0,i} &= -k\Psi_{1,i} - \dot{\Phi}\frac{\partial\ln f^{(0)}_{X,i}}{\partial\ln q}
                       - \Gamma(\Psi_{0,i} - \Psi_{0,j}), \\
    \dot{\Psi}_{1,i} &= \frac{k}{3}\left(\Psi_{0,i} - 2\Psi_{2,i}\right)
                       + k\Psi
                       - \Gamma(\Psi_{1,i} - \Psi_{1,j}), \\
    \dot{\Psi}_{2,i} &= \frac{k}{5}\left(2\Psi_{1,i} - 3\Psi_{3,i}\right)
                       - \Gamma(\Psi_{2,i} - \Psi_{2,j})
                       - \dot{\Phi}\frac{\partial\ln f^{(0)}_{X,i}}{\partial\ln q},
\end{align}
and for $\ell \geq 3$,
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\Psi}_{\ell,i}
    = \frac{k}{2\ell+1}\left[\ell\Psi_{\ell-1,i} - (\ell+1)\Psi_{\ell+1,i}\right]
      - \Gamma(\Psi_{\ell,i} - \Psi_{\ell,j}).
\end{equation}

The relaxation terms act as a multipole-by-multipole friction between the two sectors.

\subsection*{4.3 Photon and Baryon Coupling}

Photon–baryon interactions remain standard:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\theta}_{\gamma,i} = k^2\left(\frac{\delta_{\gamma,i}}{4} - \sigma_{\gamma,i}\right)
                             + a n_{e,i}\sigma_T(\theta_{b,i} - \theta_{\gamma,i})
                             - \Gamma(\theta_{\gamma,i} - \theta_{\gamma,j}),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\theta}_{b,i} = -H\theta_{b,i} + c_{s,b,i}^2 k^2\delta_{b,i}
                         + a n_{e,i}\sigma_T(\theta_{\gamma,i} - \theta_{b,i})
                         - \Gamma(\theta_{b,i} - \theta_{b,j}).
\end{equation}

Relaxation introduces a new momentum-exchange channel between the two sectors.

\subsection*{4.4 Neutrino Hierarchy}

Neutrinos free-stream in both sectors. Their hierarchy is identical to the general scalar 
hierarchy above, with the addition of sector-specific temperatures $T_{\nu,i}$ and sound 
speeds $c_{s,\nu,i}$.

Relaxation modifies the neutrino anisotropic stress:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{\nu,i} \propto \Psi_{2,i}
    \quad\Rightarrow\quad
    \dot{\sigma}_{\nu,i} \sim -\Gamma(\sigma_{\nu,i} - \sigma_{\nu,j}).
\end{equation}

This affects the CMB damping tail and the tensor transfer function.

\subsection*{4.5 Tensor Boltzmann Hierarchy}

Tensor perturbations couple to the quadrupole of each species. The tensor hierarchy becomes
\begin{align}
    \dot{F}_{\gamma,i}^{(T)} &= -k F_{\gamma,i}^{(T)} - \frac{8}{15}k\sigma_{\gamma,i}
                               - \Gamma(F_{\gamma,i}^{(T)} - F_{\gamma,j}^{(T)}), \\
    \dot{F}_{\nu,i}^{(T)} &= -k F_{\nu,i}^{(T)} - \frac{8}{15}k\sigma_{\nu,i}
                            - \Gamma(F_{\nu,i}^{(T)} - F_{\nu,j}^{(T)}),
\end{align}
with the tensor metric perturbation obeying
\begin{equation}
    \ddot{h} + (3H + \Gamma)\dot{h} + \frac{k^2}{a^2}h
    = 8\pi G a^2 \sum_i (\rho_i + p_i)\sigma_i.
\end{equation}

\subsection*{4.6 Closure Relations}

As in standard cosmology, the hierarchy must be truncated at finite $\ell_{\max}$. The 
relaxation mechanism modifies the closure relation:
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{\ell_{\max}+1,i}
    \simeq \frac{2\ell_{\max}+1}{k\tau}
           \Psi_{\ell_{\max},i}
           - \frac{2\ell_{\max}-1}{k\tau}
           \Psi_{\ell_{\max}-1,i}
           - \Gamma\tau(\Psi_{\ell_{\max},i} - \Psi_{\ell_{\max},j}).
\end{equation}

This ensures numerical stability even when $\Gamma \gtrsim H$.

\subsection*{4.7 Summary of the RDCC Boltzmann Hierarchy}

The RDCC Boltzmann hierarchy differs from the standard one in three essential ways:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sectoral duplication:}  
    Each species has two distribution functions, one per sector.

    \item \textbf{Relaxation coupling:}  
    Multipole-by-multipole friction terms couple the two sectors.

    \item \textbf{Modified tensor dynamics:}  
    Tensor modes include an additional damping term proportional to $\Gamma$.
\end{itemize}

These equations form the core of the RDCC Boltzmann Code. In the next section, we develop the 
numerical implementation strategy and the CLASS-compatible structure.

\section{Numerical Implementation and CLASS-Compatible Structure}

The RDCC Boltzmann Code must be implemented in a modular and numerically stable way. In this 
section we outline a CLASS-compatible structure that incorporates the relaxation mechanism, 
the bipartite sectoral dynamics, and the modified perturbation equations derived in the 
previous sections. The goal is to provide a blueprint that can be implemented either as a 
standalone code or as a fork of \textsc{CLASS}.

\subsection*{5.1 Modular Architecture}

The code is organized into the following modules:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{background:}  
    Computes $H(a)$, $\rho_i(a)$, $p_i(a)$, $T_i(a)$, $w_i(a)$, and $\alpha(a)$.

    \item \textbf{thermodynamics:}  
    Computes recombination, opacity, sound speeds, and sectoral thermodynamic quantities.

    \item \textbf{perturbations:}  
    Evolves scalar, vector, and tensor perturbations for both sectors.

    \item \textbf{boltzmann\_hierarchy:}  
    Implements the RDCC multipole hierarchy with relaxation coupling.

    \item \textbf{transfer:}  
    Computes transfer functions for matter, photons, neutrinos, and tensor modes.

    \item \textbf{spectra:}  
    Computes $C_\ell$, $P(k)$, $f\sigma_8$, and $\Omega_{\mathrm{GW}}(f)$.

    \item \textbf{input/output:}  
    Handles parameter input, initial conditions, and output formatting.
\end{itemize}

This structure mirrors the modularity of \textsc{CLASS}, ensuring compatibility and 
maintainability.

\subsection*{5.2 Background Module}

The background module integrates the modified continuity equations,
\begin{align}
    \dot{\rho}_A + 3H(\rho_A + p_A) &= -\Gamma(\rho_A - \rho_B), \\
    \dot{\rho}_B + 3H(\rho_B + p_B) &= +\Gamma(\rho_A - \rho_B),
\end{align}
together with the Friedmann equation,
\begin{equation}
    H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}(\rho_A + \rho_B).
\end{equation}

The module stores:

\begin{itemize}
    \item sectoral temperatures $T_A(a)$, $T_B(a)$,
    \item relaxation coefficient $\alpha(a)$,
    \item sound speeds $c_{s,i}^2(a)$,
    \item effective equations of state $w_i^{\mathrm{eff}}(a)$.
\end{itemize}

These quantities are interpolated for use in the perturbation module.

\subsection*{5.3 Perturbation Module}

The perturbation module evolves the coupled system of scalar, vector, and tensor perturbations 
for both sectors. The state vector contains:

\begin{itemize}
    \item density contrasts $\delta_A$, $\delta_B$,
    \item velocity divergences $\theta_A$, $\theta_B$,
    \item anisotropic stresses $\sigma_A$, $\sigma_B$,
    \item photon and neutrino multipoles for both sectors,
    \item baryon and dark matter perturbations,
    \item metric potentials $\Phi$ and $\Psi$,
    \item tensor modes $h$ and $\dot{h}$.
\end{itemize}

The relaxation terms appear as additional source terms in the ODE system.

\subsection*{5.4 Boltzmann Hierarchy Module}

The hierarchy module implements the multipole evolution equations,
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\Psi}_{\ell,i}
    = \frac{k}{2\ell+1}\left[\ell\Psi_{\ell-1,i} - (\ell+1)\Psi_{\ell+1,i}\right]
      - \Gamma(\Psi_{\ell,i} - \Psi_{\ell,j}),
\end{equation}
for photons and neutrinos in both sectors.

The hierarchy is truncated at $\ell_{\max}$ with the modified closure relation,
\begin{equation}
    \Psi_{\ell_{\max}+1,i}
    \simeq \frac{2\ell_{\max}+1}{k\tau}\Psi_{\ell_{\max},i}
           - \frac{2\ell_{\max}-1}{k\tau}\Psi_{\ell_{\max}-1,i}
           - \Gamma\tau(\Psi_{\ell_{\max},i} - \Psi_{\ell_{\max},j}).
\end{equation}

This ensures numerical stability even when $\Gamma \gtrsim H$.

\subsection*{5.5 Initial Conditions}

Initial conditions are set deep in the radiation era. For adiabatic initial conditions,
\begin{equation}
    \delta_A = \delta_B = \delta_\gamma = \delta_\nu,
\end{equation}
but the relaxation mechanism introduces a small sectoral offset,
\begin{equation}
    \delta_A - \delta_B \propto \alpha(a_{\mathrm{ini}}).
\end{equation}

Tensor modes start with
\begin{equation}
    h = h_0, \qquad \dot{h} = 0,
\end{equation}
with relaxation modifying the early-time damping.

\subsection*{5.6 Numerical Integration Strategy}

The ODE system is stiff when $\Gamma \gg H$. We therefore recommend:

\begin{itemize}
    \item implicit or semi-implicit integrators,
    \item adaptive step size control,
    \item sectoral variable grouping to minimize stiffness,
    \item precomputed interpolation tables for $\alpha(a)$ and $T_i(a)$.
\end{itemize}

The hierarchy is evolved in $k$-space for each mode independently.

\subsection*{5.7 CLASS-Compatible Structure}

To integrate RDCC into \textsc{CLASS}, the following modifications are required:

\begin{itemize}
    \item duplicate photon, neutrino, baryon, and dark matter species for sector~B,
    \item add relaxation terms to the perturbation and hierarchy modules,
    \item modify the background module to include sectoral temperatures and energy exchange,
    \item extend the transfer module to compute sectoral contributions,
    \item add RDCC-specific parameters to the input parser.
\end{itemize}

The resulting structure remains fully compatible with the \textsc{CLASS} pipeline for 
computing $C_\ell$, $P(k)$, and derived observables.

\subsection*{5.8 Summary}

The numerical implementation of the RDCC Boltzmann Code requires:

\begin{itemize}
    \item a modular architecture mirroring \textsc{CLASS},
    \item sectoral duplication of species,
    \item relaxation-induced coupling terms,
    \item modified closure relations,
    \item stiff ODE solvers for stability.
\end{itemize}

This framework enables the computation of all cosmological observables within RDCC, forming 
the computational backbone for future Companions.
