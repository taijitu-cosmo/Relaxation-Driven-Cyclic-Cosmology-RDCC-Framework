\begin{abstract}
Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) predicts a distinctive tensor signature arising from the CPT-symmetric structure of the Universe. Unlike inflationary scenarios, tensor perturbations in RDCC are not generated by stochastic amplification at horizon exit, but emerge from CPT-consistent matching of tensor modes across a nonsingular cuscuton-induced bounce. The bipartite Hilbert-space structure of RDCC implies that visible and mirror tensor sectors interfere, producing a log-periodic modulation of the stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB). This modulation has the universal form


\[
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) \propto \alpha^2 \left[ 1 + \varepsilon \cos\!\left( \alpha \ln\!\frac{f}{f_b} + \phi \right) \right],
\]


where $\alpha$ is the relaxation parameter, $f_b$ is the bounce scale, and $(\varepsilon,\phi)$ encode the CPT-induced phase structure. The modulation period $\Delta \ln f = 2\pi/\alpha$ is fixed by the one-parameter nature of RDCC. This paper develops the full derivation of the tensor equations, the bounce matching conditions, the origin of the interference term, and the resulting observational consequences for LISA, DECIGO, BBO, Einstein Telescope, Cosmic Explorer, and CMB-S4. The resulting tensor phenomenology provides a sharply falsifiable prediction and constitutes the central observational target of RDCC.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) is a one-parameter, CPT-symmetric framework in which the Universe evolves through a nonsingular bounce connecting a contracting and an expanding phase. The global quantum state is a CPT eigenstate, implying a bipartite Hilbert-space factorization into a visible sector and its CPT-conjugate mirror sector. Gravity couples to the sum of the two stress-energy tensors, while a unique dimension-six operator induces a small relaxation parameter $\alpha \sim 10^{-2}$ that governs correlated deviations from $\Lambda$CDM.

RDCC predicts a nearly scale-invariant scalar spectrum with $n_s - 1 = -2\alpha$, a dark-radiation excess $\Delta N_{\rm eff} \sim \alpha$, percent-level growth-rate deviations, and a blue-tilted stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB) peaked in the millihertz range. A distinctive feature of RDCC is the presence of log-periodic oscillations in the tensor spectrum, arising from interference between the two CPT-related tensor sectors across the bounce. These oscillations constitute a smoking-gun signature of RDCC and provide a unique observational target for next-generation gravitational-wave detectors.

The purpose of this Companion Paper is to develop the full tensor sector of RDCC, including the derivation of the tensor equations, the bounce matching conditions, the origin of the log-periodic modulation, and the resulting observational phenomenology. This analysis builds on the formal gravitational framework established in Companion~I and the cosmological structure developed in RDCC~v35.0.


\section{Tensor Equations in a CPT-Symmetric Two-Sector Universe}

The tensor sector of RDCC inherits its structure from the bipartite Hilbert-space factorization and the global Einstein equations. Gravity couples to the sum of the two stress-energy tensors,
\begin{equation}
G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi G_N \left( T^{(+)}_{\mu\nu} + T^{(-)}_{\mu\nu} \right),
\end{equation}
while gauge interactions remain strictly sectoral. Tensor perturbations therefore propagate on a single shared metric but receive contributions from both sectors.

We decompose the metric as
\begin{equation}
g_{\mu\nu} = \bar{g}_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu},
\end{equation}
where $\bar{g}_{\mu\nu}$ is the spatially flat FLRW background and $h_{\mu\nu}$ is a transverse-traceless tensor perturbation satisfying
\begin{equation}
\nabla^i h_{ij} = 0, \qquad h^i{}_i = 0.
\end{equation}

Linearizing the global Einstein equations yields
\begin{equation}
\delta G_{ij} = 8\pi G_N \left( \delta T^{(+)}_{ij} + \delta T^{(-)}_{ij} \right).
\end{equation}
For tensor modes, the anisotropic stress is the only relevant contribution,
\begin{equation}
\delta T^{(\pm)}_{ij} = \pi^{(\pm)}_{ij}.
\end{equation}

The global tensor equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_{ij} + 3H \dot{h}_{ij} - \frac{\nabla^2}{a^2} h_{ij}
= 16\pi G_N \left( \pi^{(+)}_{ij} + \pi^{(-)}_{ij} \right).
\label{eq:global_tensor}
\end{equation}

A visible-sector observer perceives only the projection onto $H_+$, which yields
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}^{(+)}_{ij} + 3H \dot{h}^{(+)}_{ij} - \frac{\nabla^2}{a^2} h^{(+)}_{ij}
= 16\pi G_{\rm eff}\, \pi^{(+)}_{ij},
\label{eq:sectoral_tensor}
\end{equation}
where the effective Newton constant is
\begin{equation}
G_{\rm eff} = \frac{G_N}{1 + \rho_-/\rho_+}.
\end{equation}

\section{Derivation of Log-Periodic Modulation}

The log-periodic modulation of the stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB) is a direct consequence of the CPT-symmetric two-sector structure of RDCC. The key ingredients are: (i) the global tensor equation, (ii) the bounce matching conditions, and (iii) the CPT relation between the visible and mirror tensor modes. Together, these imply that the tensor spectrum acquires a discrete scale invariance, leading to oscillations in $\ln k$.

We begin by writing the tensor perturbation in Fourier space,
\begin{equation}
h_{ij}(t,\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{\lambda=+,\times} \int \frac{d^3 k}{(2\pi)^3} \, h_\lambda(t,k) \, e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \, e^{(\lambda)}_{ij}(\mathbf{k}),
\end{equation}
where $e^{(\lambda)}_{ij}$ are the polarization tensors. The mode functions satisfy
\begin{equation}
\ddot{h}_\lambda + 3H \dot{h}_\lambda + \frac{k^2}{a^2} h_\lambda
= 16\pi G_N \left( \pi^{(+)}_\lambda + \pi^{(-)}_\lambda \right).
\label{eq:tensor_mode_eq}
\end{equation}

Near the bounce, the Hubble parameter vanishes and the relaxation parameter approaches zero,
\begin{equation}
H(t_b) = 0, \qquad \alpha(t_b) \to 0,
\end{equation}
so the two sectors become maximally correlated. The general solution in the contracting phase ($t<t_b$) may be written as
\begin{equation}
h_\lambda^{\rm (con)}(k,t) = A_\lambda(k) e^{-i\omega \ln(k/k_b)} + B_\lambda(k) e^{+i\omega \ln(k/k_b)},
\label{eq:con_solution}
\end{equation}
where $\omega$ is a dimensionless frequency determined by the relaxation dynamics. The logarithmic dependence arises because the effective mass term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:tensor_mode_eq} scales as $k^2/a^2$, and the scale factor evolves approximately exponentially in conformal time near the bounce.

In the expanding phase ($t>t_b$), the solution takes the form
\begin{equation}
h_\lambda^{\rm (exp)}(k,t) = C_\lambda(k) e^{-i\omega \ln(k/k_b)} + D_\lambda(k) e^{+i\omega \ln(k/k_b)}.
\label{eq:exp_solution}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
h_\lambda^{(+)}(t,k) = h_\lambda^{(-)}(-t,k),

\section{Amplitude and Blue Tilt}

RDCC predicts a blue-tilted stochastic gravitational-wave background, with the amplitude controlled by the relaxation parameter $\alpha$. The global tensor equation implies that the SGWB amplitude scales as
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) \sim \alpha^2 \, \Omega_0(f),
\end{equation}
where $\Omega_0(f)$ is the reference spectrum in the absence of inter-sector effects. This quadratic dependence arises because the tensor amplitude receives contributions from both sectors, and the interference term is proportional to the square of the relaxation-induced phase shift.

The blue tilt is a consequence of the cuscuton-induced bounce. Near the bounce, the effective equation of state becomes stiff, leading to an enhancement of high-frequency modes. The resulting spectrum takes the approximate form
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) \propto f^{n_T},
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
n_T > 0,
\end{equation}
in contrast to the nearly scale-invariant or red-tilted spectra typical of inflationary models.

The combination of a blue tilt and log-periodic modulation produces a distinctive SGWB signature:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) = \alpha^2 f^{n_T}
\left[ 1 + \varepsilon \cos\!\left( \alpha \ln\!\frac{f}{f_b} + \phi \right) \right].
\end{equation}

\section{Observational Forecasts}

The tensor signature of RDCC is characterized by three structural features: a blue-tilted spectrum, a millihertz-scale peak, and a log-periodic modulation with period $\Delta \ln f = 2\pi/\alpha$. These features span more than twenty decades in frequency and are accessible to a broad range of gravitational-wave experiments. This section summarizes the observational prospects for detecting each component of the RDCC tensor signal.

\subsection{CMB Experiments: Low-Frequency Tail}

At the lowest frequencies, the RDCC tensor spectrum contributes to the CMB B-mode polarization. The log-periodic modulation is strongly suppressed in this regime, but the overall amplitude and tilt remain sensitive to the relaxation parameter. Experiments such as LiteBIRD and CMB-S4 can probe the low-frequency tail of the spectrum and constrain the combination
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f_{\rm CMB}) \sim \alpha^2 f_{\rm CMB}^{n_T}.
\end{equation}
A detection of a blue-tilted tensor spectrum at CMB scales would be incompatible with standard inflationary predictions and would provide indirect support for RDCC.

\subsection{LISA: Millihertz Peak and Log-Periodic Modulation}

The peak of the RDCC tensor spectrum lies naturally in the millihertz band,
\begin{equation}
f_b \sim \mathcal{O}({\rm mHz}),
\end{equation}
making LISA the prime detector for the RDCC signal. The log-periodic modulation is most pronounced near the peak, where the interference between the visible and mirror sectors is strongest. LISA's broad frequency coverage and high sensitivity allow it to probe both the amplitude and the oscillatory structure of the spectrum.

The modulation period,
\begin{equation}
\Delta \ln f = \frac{2\pi}{\alpha} \approx 360,
\end{equation}
is large for $\alpha \approx 0.0175$, implying that only a fraction of a full oscillation is visible within the LISA band. Nevertheless, the presence of even a partial oscillation would constitute a distinctive signature.

\subsection{DECIGO and BBO: Broadband Modulation}

Future space-based detectors such as DECIGO and BBO offer significantly broader frequency coverage than LISA. Their sensitivity to the mid-frequency regime allows them to probe multiple cycles of the log-periodic modulation. This makes them ideal instruments for testing the discrete scale invariance predicted by RDCC.

A detection of repeated oscillations in $\ln f$ would provide a direct measurement of the relaxation parameter $\alpha$ and would serve as a definitive test of the RDCC framework.

\subsection{Ground-Based Detectors: High-Frequency Tail}

Ground-based interferometers such as the Einstein Telescope (ET) and Cosmic Explorer (CE) probe the high-frequency tail of the RDCC spectrum. In this regime, the blue tilt enhances the amplitude relative to inflationary predictions. Although the log-periodic modulation is less pronounced at high frequencies, the overall spectral shape remains a powerful discriminator.

The combination of LISA, DECIGO/BBO, and ET/CE provides a multi-band observational strategy capable of testing the full RDCC tensor signature across more than twenty decades in frequency.


\section{Falsifiability}

RDCC is a sharply falsifiable framework. Its one-parameter structure implies that all deviations from $\Lambda$CDM are correlated and controlled by the relaxation parameter $\alpha$. The tensor sector provides the most distinctive and model-specific predictions, making it the primary arena for falsification.

\subsection{Absence of Log-Periodic Modulation}

The presence of log-periodic oscillations in the SGWB is a direct consequence of the CPT-symmetric two-sector structure. If future gravitational-wave observations reveal a smooth tensor spectrum without oscillatory features, RDCC would be ruled out.

\subsection{Inconsistent Amplitude Scaling}

The SGWB amplitude scales as $\alpha^2$. A measured amplitude inconsistent with the value of $\alpha$ inferred from the scalar spectral tilt,
\begin{equation}
n_s - 1 = -2\alpha,
\end{equation}
would falsify RDCC.

\subsection{Incorrect Peak Frequency}

The peak frequency $f_b$ is determined by the bounce scale and lies naturally in the millihertz range. A significantly different peak frequency would be incompatible with the cuscuton-induced bounce dynamics.

\subsection{Detection of Non-Gravitational Dark Matter}

RDCC predicts that dark matter is entirely gravitational and mirror-baryonic. The detection of any non-gravitational dark matter candidate (e.g., WIMPs, axions with electromagnetic couplings) would falsify the RDCC interpretation of dark matter as a gravitational shadow.

\subsection{Violation of Correlated Predictions}

The one-parameter nature of RDCC implies the following correlations:
\begin{equation}
n_s - 1 = -2\alpha, \qquad \Delta N_{\rm eff} \sim \alpha, \qquad \Omega_{\rm GW} \propto \alpha^2.
\end{equation}
Any inconsistency among these predictions would rule out the framework.

The tensor sector therefore provides a decisive and experimentally accessible test of RDCC. The detection of log-periodic modulation in the SGWB would constitute strong evidence for the CPT-symmetric two-sector structure, while its absence would exclude the model.

\section{Conclusion}

The tensor sector of Relaxation-Driven Cyclic Cosmology (RDCC) provides a uniquely sharp and experimentally accessible window into the CPT-symmetric structure of the Universe. Unlike inflationary models, where tensor modes originate from quantum fluctuations amplified at horizon exit, RDCC generates tensor perturbations through CPT-consistent matching across a nonsingular cuscuton-induced bounce. The bipartite Hilbert-space structure implies that visible and mirror tensor sectors interfere, producing a log-periodic modulation of the stochastic gravitational-wave background (SGWB).

The resulting tensor spectrum,
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{\rm GW}(f) = \alpha^2 f^{n_T}
\left[ 1 + \varepsilon \cos\!\left( \alpha \ln\!\frac{f}{f_b} + \phi \right) \right],
\end{equation}
encodes three structural predictions: a blue tilt, a millihertz-scale peak, and a log-periodic modulation with period $\Delta \ln f = 2\pi/\alpha$. Each of these features arises directly from the one-parameter nature of RDCC and the global CPT symmetry of the cosmological state.

The observational prospects are exceptionally strong. LISA probes the peak region, DECIGO and BBO can resolve multiple oscillation cycles, and ground-based detectors test the high-frequency tail. CMB experiments constrain the low-frequency limit. Together, these instruments provide a multi-band strategy capable of confirming or falsifying the RDCC tensor signature.

The tensor sector therefore stands as the decisive experimental test of RDCC. A detection of log-periodic modulation would constitute compelling evidence for a CPT-symmetric two-sector Universe, while its absence would rule out the framework. The next decade of gravitational-wave astronomy will thus play a central role in determining whether the Universe is fundamentally cyclic, globally symmetric, and governed by a single relaxation parameter.
