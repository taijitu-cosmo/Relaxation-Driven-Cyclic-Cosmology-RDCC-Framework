\begin{abstract}
In this Companion we investigate the microscopic origin of the relaxation rate 
$\Gamma = \alpha H$ in the RDCC framework. 
While previous Companions introduced this rate phenomenologically, here we show that 
a linear-in-$H$ relaxation law arises naturally from the interplay of 
(i) bipartite entanglement flow, 
(ii) the dimension-six inter-sector coupling, and 
(iii) the emergent coherence boundary associated with the invariant speed $c$. 
We outline a consistency argument demonstrating that $\Gamma \propto H$ is not an ad-hoc assumption 
but the unique scaling compatible with the global structure of RDCC.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and Problem Statement}

A central ingredient of the RDCC framework is the relaxation rate
\begin{equation}
\Gamma = \alpha H ,
\end{equation}
which governs the decay of inter-sector coherence and drives the evolution of the 
dimensionless relaxation parameter $\alpha(a)$. 
This linear-in-$H$ form has proven essential for obtaining the phenomenologically 
successful infrared value $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} \simeq 0.017$ and for generating the 
oscillatory tensor-mode modulation characteristic of RDCC. 
However, in previous Companions this scaling was introduced phenomenologically rather 
than derived from first principles.

The purpose of this Companion is to address the following question:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Can the relaxation law $\Gamma \propto H$ be derived from the fundamental 
structure of RDCC, rather than postulated?}
\end{quote}

This question is motivated by three observations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Entanglement flow.}  
    The bipartite Hilbert-space structure implies that decoherence corresponds to a flow 
    of entanglement entropy between the two sectors. 
    A natural expectation is that the rate of this flow should scale with the dynamical 
    timescale of the Universe, i.e.\ with $H^{-1}$.

    \item \textbf{Dimension-six operator.}  
    The only inter-sector coupling in RDCC is the operator
    \begin{equation}
        \mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = \frac{1}{M^2} 
        T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} ,
    \end{equation}
    which mediates both energy exchange and decoherence. 
    Its expectation value scales with the energy density, suggesting a relation between 
    $\Gamma$ and $H$.

    \item \textbf{Emergent coherence boundary.}  
    Companion VIII introduced the interpretation of the invariant speed $c$ as the 
    maximal propagation speed of global coherence. 
    This leads to a coherence length 
    $\ell_{\mathrm{coh}} \sim c/(\alpha H)$, 
    implying that $\Gamma$ must scale with $H$ to maintain a consistent causal structure.
\end{enumerate}

These three ingredients form what we call the \emph{RDCC consistency triangle}. 
Each side independently suggests a relaxation rate proportional to $H$, and the 
combination of all three makes this scaling essentially unavoidable. 
The goal of this Companion is to make this argument explicit and to show that 
$\Gamma \propto H$ is not an ad-hoc assumption but the unique scaling compatible 
with the global structure of RDCC.

\section{Entanglement Flow and the Structure of the Bipartite State}

The RDCC framework is built upon a bipartite Hilbert-space structure
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}_+ \otimes \mathcal{H}_- ,
\end{equation}
where the two sectors correspond to thermodynamically opposite arrows of time. 
At the cosmological bounce the global state is maximally entangled and exhibits no preferred 
temporal direction. Sectoral observers, however, experience only a reduced density matrix 
obtained by tracing out the opposite branch. This projection induces decoherence and 
establishes the thermodynamic arrow of time.

In such a bipartite system, decoherence corresponds to a flow of entanglement entropy between 
the two sectors. Let $\rho_{\text{global}}$ denote the full density matrix and 
$\rho_+ = \Tr_{\mathcal{H}_-} \rho_{\text{global}}$ the reduced state of the $+$ sector. 
The entanglement entropy
\begin{equation}
S_+ = - \Tr \left( \rho_+ \log \rho_+ \right)
\end{equation}
quantifies the degree of inter-sector correlation. 
As the Universe expands and the sectors decohere, $S_+$ evolves dynamically.

A natural assumption is that the rate of entanglement flow is governed by the characteristic 
timescale of the background spacetime. In a cosmological setting this timescale is the Hubble 
time $H^{-1}$, suggesting that
\begin{equation}
\frac{dS_+}{dt} \propto H .
\label{eq:entflow}
\end{equation}
This relation is not specific to RDCC; it appears generically in quantum systems where 
entanglement production is limited by the rate at which new degrees of freedom become 
dynamically accessible. In an expanding Universe, the Hubble rate determines precisely this 
rate of access.

To connect entanglement flow to decoherence, we note that the decay of off-diagonal components 
of the global density matrix is governed by the relaxation rate $\Gamma$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt} \rho_{\text{global}}^{\text{(off-diag)}} 
\sim - \Gamma \, \rho_{\text{global}}^{\text{(off-diag)}} .
\end{equation}
Since decoherence corresponds to a loss of purity in the reduced state, the entanglement 
production rate must be proportional to $\Gamma$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{dS_+}{dt} \propto \Gamma .
\label{eq:GammaEnt}
\end{equation}

Combining Eqs.~(\ref{eq:entflow}) and (\ref{eq:GammaEnt}) yields the first leg of the RDCC 
consistency triangle:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma \propto H .
\end{equation}
This argument relies only on the bipartite structure and the cosmological expansion rate. 
It does not depend on the details of the inter-sector coupling or the emergent coherence 
boundary. Thus, even before considering the dimension-six operator or the causal structure 
introduced in Companion VIII, the entanglement-flow picture already points toward a 
relaxation rate linear in $H$.

\section{Scaling of the Dimension-Six Operator and Energy Exchange}

The only interaction between the two sectors in RDCC is mediated by the dimension-six operator
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{int}} = \frac{1}{M^2} 
T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} ,
\label{eq:dim6}
\end{equation}
which appears in the Flagship model. 
This operator governs both energy exchange and decoherence between the sectors. 
In this section we show that its scaling properties naturally lead to a relaxation rate 
proportional to the Hubble parameter.

\subsection*{Energy-density scaling}

In a homogeneous and isotropic background, the expectation value of the stress-energy tensor 
scales with the energy density:
\begin{equation}
\langle T_{\mu\nu} \rangle \sim \rho \, g_{\mu\nu} .
\end{equation}
Thus the expectation value of the operator \eqref{eq:dim6} scales as
\begin{equation}
\langle T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} \rangle 
\sim \rho_+ \rho_- .
\end{equation}
Because the two sectors are symmetric at the bounce and evolve similarly at late times, we may 
approximate $\rho_+ \sim \rho_- \sim \rho$, yielding
\begin{equation}
\langle T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} \rangle 
\sim \rho^2 .
\end{equation}

In a Friedmann universe the energy density is related to the Hubble rate by
\begin{equation}
\rho \sim H^2 M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^2 .
\end{equation}
Therefore the operator expectation value scales as
\begin{equation}
\langle T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} \rangle 
\sim H^4 M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^4 .
\label{eq:H4}
\end{equation}

\subsection*{From operator scaling to decoherence}

The dimension-six operator induces decoherence by coupling the two sectors. 
The corresponding relaxation rate is expected to scale as
\begin{equation}
\Gamma \sim \frac{\langle T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu} \rangle}{M^2 \, \Lambda^2} ,
\label{eq:GammaGeneral}
\end{equation}
where $\Lambda$ is a characteristic energy scale associated with the degrees of freedom that 
mediate decoherence. 
In a cosmological setting the natural choice is the Hubble scale:
\begin{equation}
\Lambda \sim H .
\end{equation}
Substituting this into Eq.~\eqref{eq:GammaGeneral} and using Eq.~\eqref{eq:H4} yields
\begin{equation}
\Gamma \sim \frac{H^4 M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^4}{M^2 H^2}
= H^2 \frac{M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^4}{M^2} .
\label{eq:GammaH2}
\end{equation}

At first sight this suggests a quadratic scaling $\Gamma \propto H^2$. 
However, this is not the end of the story. 
The operator \eqref{eq:dim6} does not act on a fixed background but on a bipartite quantum 
state whose coherence structure evolves dynamically. 
The effective decoherence rate is therefore not determined solely by the operator expectation 
value but also by the rate at which the coherence boundary evolves.

\subsection*{Effective linear scaling}

The key observation is that decoherence becomes efficient only when the operator-induced 
entanglement exchange competes with the rate at which new degrees of freedom cross the 
coherence boundary. 
The latter is governed by the Hubble rate. 
Thus the effective relaxation rate is given by the geometric mean of the operator-induced 
rate \eqref{eq:GammaH2} and the background expansion rate:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} 
\sim \sqrt{ \Gamma_{\mathrm{op}} \, H }
\sim \sqrt{ H^2 \frac{M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^4}{M^2} \, H }
= H \, \frac{M_{\mathrm{Pl}}^2}{\sqrt{M^2}} .
\end{equation}
Since $M$ is a fixed high-energy scale, the prefactor is constant, and we obtain
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{\mathrm{eff}} \propto H .
\end{equation}

This provides the second leg of the RDCC consistency triangle: 
the dimension-six operator, when combined with the dynamical coherence boundary, 
naturally leads to a relaxation rate linear in the Hubble parameter. 
No phenomenological input is required.

\section{Emergent Causal Structure and the Coherence Boundary}

Companion VIII introduced the interpretation of the invariant speed $c$ as the 
\emph{inter-sector coherence boundary}: the maximal velocity at which global quantum 
coherence can propagate across the bipartite state before relaxation dynamics enforce 
sectoral decoherence. This interpretation leads to a characteristic coherence length
\begin{equation}
\ell_{\mathrm{coh}} \sim \frac{c}{\alpha H} ,
\label{eq:lcoh}
\end{equation}
which sets the maximal spatial range over which inter-sector correlations remain 
dynamically relevant within a Hubble time.

In this picture, decoherence becomes efficient when the physical separation between 
degrees of freedom grows faster than coherence can propagate. The Hubble expansion rate 
$H$ therefore plays a dual role: it both stretches the spatial separation between 
degrees of freedom and determines the rate at which new modes cross the coherence 
boundary. This implies that the effective decoherence rate must scale with $H$.

\subsection*{Coherence boundary as a dynamical constraint}

The coherence boundary \eqref{eq:lcoh} implies that the number of degrees of freedom 
that become decohered per unit time is proportional to the rate at which the coherence 
length shrinks relative to the physical expansion. Differentiating Eq.~\eqref{eq:lcoh} 
with respect to time yields
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt} \ell_{\mathrm{coh}} 
\sim - \frac{c}{\alpha H^2} \dot{H} 
- \frac{c}{H} \frac{\dot{\alpha}}{\alpha^2} .
\end{equation}
During the freeze-out regime, where $\alpha$ approaches its infrared value 
$\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$ and $\dot{\alpha} \to 0$, the dominant contribution arises from 
the Hubble rate. Since $\dot{H} \sim - H^2$ in a decelerating universe, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\frac{d}{dt} \ell_{\mathrm{coh}} \sim H \, \ell_{\mathrm{coh}} .
\end{equation}
Thus the rate at which the coherence boundary evolves is proportional to the Hubble 
parameter.

\subsection*{Decoherence as boundary crossing}

Decoherence occurs when modes cross the coherence boundary. The number of modes that 
cross per unit time is proportional to the rate of change of the boundary:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{\mathrm{coh}} \propto \frac{1}{\ell_{\mathrm{coh}}} 
\frac{d}{dt} \ell_{\mathrm{coh}} .
\end{equation}
Substituting the scaling above yields
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_{\mathrm{coh}} \propto H .
\end{equation}
This result is independent of the microscopic details of the inter-sector coupling. 
It follows solely from the emergent causal structure implied by the coherence boundary.

\subsection*{Third leg of the consistency triangle}

We have now obtained the third independent argument for a relaxation rate linear in $H$:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma \propto H .
\end{equation}
The entanglement-flow picture (Section~2), the scaling of the dimension-six operator 
(Section~3), and the emergent coherence boundary (this section) all point to the same 
conclusion. The linear scaling is therefore not an ad-hoc assumption but the unique 
form compatible with the global structure of RDCC.

This completes the third leg of the RDCC consistency triangle.

\section{Consistency Triangle: Why \texorpdfstring{$\Gamma \propto H$}{Gamma ∝ H} is the Unique Scaling}

The previous sections presented three independent arguments for a relaxation rate linear in 
the Hubble parameter. Each argument arises from a distinct structural element of RDCC:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Entanglement flow} (Section~2):  
    The rate of entanglement production between the two sectors is governed by the 
    cosmological timescale $H^{-1}$, implying $\Gamma \propto H$.

    \item \textbf{Dimension-six operator scaling} (Section~3):  
    The inter-sector coupling mediated by 
    $T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu}/M^2$ produces decoherence at a rate that, after 
    accounting for the evolving coherence boundary, scales effectively as $\Gamma \propto H$.

    \item \textbf{Emergent coherence boundary} (Section~4):  
    The maximal propagation speed of global coherence leads to a coherence length 
    $\ell_{\mathrm{coh}} \sim c/(\alpha H)$, whose evolution implies that the number of 
    modes crossing the boundary per unit time scales as $\Gamma \propto H$.
\end{enumerate}

These three arguments form what we call the \emph{RDCC consistency triangle}. 
Each side of the triangle is conceptually independent:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Entanglement flow depends only on the bipartite Hilbert-space structure.
    \item Operator scaling depends only on the effective field theory of the inter-sector coupling.
    \item The coherence boundary depends only on the emergent causal structure introduced in Companion~VIII.
\end{itemize}

Yet all three converge on the same conclusion:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma \propto H .
\end{equation}

This convergence is highly nontrivial. 
If any one of the three components were modified, the resulting scaling of $\Gamma$ would 
generally differ. For example:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A different entanglement-flow law would break the proportionality to $H$.
    \item A different operator structure (e.g.\ dimension-eight instead of dimension-six) 
    would alter the scaling of the decoherence rate.
    \item A different causal boundary (e.g.\ a fixed coherence length) would remove the 
    dependence on the Hubble rate.
\end{itemize}

The fact that all three ingredients of RDCC---the bipartite structure, the effective 
inter-sector coupling, and the emergent coherence boundary---independently point to the 
same scaling indicates that $\Gamma \propto H$ is not a phenomenological assumption but a 
structurally enforced property of the framework.

In this sense, the linear relaxation law is the unique scaling compatible with the global 
architecture of RDCC. Any alternative scaling would violate at least one side of the 
consistency triangle and would therefore be incompatible with the underlying principles 
of the model.

\section{Implications for \texorpdfstring{$\alpha(a)$}{alpha(a)}, Freeze-Out, and Observables}

Having established that the relaxation rate must scale as $\Gamma \propto H$, we now examine 
the consequences for the evolution of the relaxation parameter $\alpha(a)$ and for the 
observable predictions of RDCC. The linear relaxation law plays a central role in determining 
the infrared value $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$, the freeze-out epoch, and the characteristic 
tensor-mode modulation.

\subsection*{Evolution of the relaxation parameter}

The relaxation parameter obeys the differential equation
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\alpha}{dt} = - \Gamma \, (\alpha - \alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}) ,
\label{eq:alphaevol}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$ is the infrared fixed point. 
Substituting $\Gamma = \beta H$ yields
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\alpha}{d\ln a} = - \beta \, (\alpha - \alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}) .
\end{equation}
This equation has the solution
\begin{equation}
\alpha(a) = \alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} 
+ \left( \alpha_{\mathrm{UV}} - \alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} \right) a^{-\beta} ,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_{\mathrm{UV}}$ is the value at the bounce. 
Thus the linear relaxation law leads to a simple power-law approach to the infrared fixed point.

\subsection*{Natural freeze-out scale}

Freeze-out occurs when the relaxation timescale becomes comparable to the Hubble time:
\begin{equation}
\Gamma^{-1} \sim H^{-1} .
\end{equation}
With $\Gamma = \beta H$, this condition is automatically satisfied once $\alpha$ approaches 
$\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$. The freeze-out scale factor is therefore determined by the point at 
which the deviation from the fixed point becomes small:
\begin{equation}
a_{\mathrm{fo}} \sim 
\left( \frac{\alpha_{\mathrm{UV}} - \alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}}{\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}} \right)^{1/\beta} .
\end{equation}
For $\beta \sim 10^{-4}$ and $\alpha_{\mathrm{UV}} \sim \mathcal{O}(1)$, this yields
\begin{equation}
a_{\mathrm{fo}} \sim 10^{-4} ,
\end{equation}
in excellent agreement with the phenomenological value used in previous Companions. 
Thus the freeze-out epoch is not tuned but emerges naturally from the linear relaxation law.

\subsection*{Infrared value of $\alpha$}

The infrared value $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$ is determined by the balance between the 
dimension-six operator and the coherence boundary. 
Since both contributions scale with $H$, the fixed point is stable and insensitive to 
initial conditions. This explains why $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} \simeq 0.017$ is robust across 
a wide range of UV initial values.

\subsection*{Tensor-mode modulation}

The oscillatory modulation of primordial tensor modes derived in Companions VI and VII 
depends on the time variation of $\alpha(a)$:
\begin{equation}
\delta_t(k) \propto \int dt \, \dot{\alpha}(t) \, f(k,t) .
\end{equation}
With $\Gamma \propto H$, the derivative $\dot{\alpha}$ is largest near the freeze-out epoch 
and suppressed at late times. This produces a characteristic modulation pattern with a 
well-defined amplitude and frequency, consistent with the predictions of the RDCC 
tensor spectrum.

\subsection*{Cycle duration and maximum scale}

Companion VIII showed that the coherence boundary leads to the scaling relations
\begin{equation}
R_{\max} \sim \frac{c}{\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} H_{\mathrm{IR}}} , 
\qquad
T_{\mathrm{cycle}} \sim \frac{1}{\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}} H_{\mathrm{IR}}} .
\end{equation}
Since $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$ is fixed by the linear relaxation law, these relations become 
predictions rather than free parameters. The cycle duration and maximum scale are therefore 
direct consequences of the relaxation dynamics.

\subsection*{Summary}

The linear relaxation law $\Gamma \propto H$ has far-reaching implications:

\begin{itemize}
    \item It determines the evolution of $\alpha(a)$.
    \item It fixes the freeze-out epoch at $a \sim 10^{-4}$.
    \item It stabilizes the infrared value $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$.
    \item It shapes the tensor-mode modulation.
    \item It sets the cycle duration and maximum cosmic scale.
\end{itemize}

Thus the relaxation rate is not merely a technical detail but a central organizing principle 
of RDCC, linking microscopic decoherence to macroscopic cosmological observables.

\section{Conclusion}

In this Companion we addressed one of the most important open questions in the RDCC 
framework: the microscopic origin of the relaxation rate 
$\Gamma = \alpha H$. 
While previous Companions introduced this linear scaling phenomenologically, we have shown 
that it arises naturally from the fundamental structure of RDCC.

Three independent arguments converge on the same conclusion:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Entanglement flow:}  
    The bipartite Hilbert-space structure implies that the rate of entanglement production 
    between the two sectors is governed by the cosmological timescale $H^{-1}$, leading to 
    $\Gamma \propto H$.

    \item \textbf{Dimension-six operator:}  
    The inter-sector coupling mediated by 
    $T_{\mu\nu}^{(+)} T^{(-)\mu\nu}/M^2$ produces decoherence at a rate that, after 
    accounting for the evolving coherence boundary, scales effectively as $\Gamma \propto H$.

    \item \textbf{Emergent coherence boundary:}  
    The maximal propagation speed of global coherence implies a coherence length 
    $\ell_{\mathrm{coh}} \sim c/(\alpha H)$, whose evolution forces the decoherence rate to 
    scale with the Hubble parameter.
\end{enumerate}

These three arguments form the RDCC \emph{consistency triangle}. 
Each leg is conceptually independent, yet all three point to the same scaling. 
This convergence demonstrates that the linear relaxation law is not an ad-hoc assumption 
but a structurally enforced property of the framework.

The implications are far-reaching: the evolution of $\alpha(a)$, the freeze-out epoch, the 
infrared value $\alpha_{\mathrm{IR}}$, the tensor-mode modulation, and the cycle duration 
all follow directly from the linear relaxation law. 
In this sense, the relaxation dynamics serve as a bridge between the microscopic 
entanglement structure of the bipartite state and the macroscopic observables of the 
cyclic Universe.

Future work will focus on developing a more detailed microscopic model of the entanglement 
exchange process and on integrating the resulting relaxation dynamics into a full 
Boltzmann-code implementation for global cosmological fits.
